Connections

A connection is the web a brush weaves between nearby points — the styles that used to be called “presets.” Pick one and any connecting brush wears it.

What they are

Some brushes (Round, Handfree, Soft Pencil) don't just lay a stroke — they look up neighbouring points in the memory map and draw lines between them. A connection is a named bundle of settings for those lines: their density, reach, opacity, line shape, grain and texture. The mark is made of many faint lines, so the look builds up as you cover an area.

Choose a connection from the navbar Connecting combo (next to the brush selector); open its gear for the dials. The dials a style actually uses show by default — the rest fold under More. See Memory maps & connecting for every dial and Covering bigger areas for Weight, Spread & Dynamics.

Classic

The default sketchy web (after mrdoob's Harmony “sketchy” brush): faint, brush-width lines, slightly inset so they float between points rather than piling up on the dots. The everyday connecting look — start here, then raise Weight for a bolder mark made of more thin hairs.

Web →

A sparser, slightly stronger net of full-length lines (Harmony's “web”): distinct strands stretched between points. Great for spider-webs, nets and wireframe-like fills.

Arc

Bulging arcs between points instead of straight lines — each connection bows outward into a curve. Reads as scallops, petals and looping ribbon; pairs well with a dotted dash and some inset (the basis of Lace).

Shaded →

Dense, short, full-length lines whose opacity fades with distance (Harmony's soft-pencil): scribble back and forth to build smooth, soft tone. The Soft Pencil brush is this style on a brush with no solid core.

Fur →

A combed pelt of soft, flowing guard hairs that lie along a grain, taper to wispy tips and fray at the edges. See Texture connections.

Lace →

A fine crossing net of floating, dotted scallops (arc connections + a dotted dash + inset ends). Best for filling a shape with lace. See Texture connections.
